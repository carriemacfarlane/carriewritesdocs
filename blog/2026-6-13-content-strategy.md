I recently passed my 3-year work anniversary and I’ve decided it’s time to update my job description. In addition to writing, editing, and mentoring, I’ve been managing terminology, creating workflows, writing user interface text, and initiating cross-team conversations about content alignment. What I’ve been doing is content strategy, but I’ve never heard that term used in my office. To get buy-in for this revision, I’ll have to talk up content strategy. I’m looking for a soundbite that explains what content strategy is.

Years ago, when I started exploring technical writing, I listened every week to a podcast from content strategist Kristina Halvorson. She talked about a content strategy “quad.” The quad was split into two halves, content and people. Substance and structure components made up the content half, and workflow and governance components made up the people half. If you pointed to the quad, you could quickly describe content strategy as a combination of content work and people work.

Kristina has shared an updated content strategy quad on her blog. The new quad highlights different kinds of work: a content design half contains editorial and experience components, and a systems design half contains structure and process components. So now, you could quickly describe content strategy as a combination of content design work and systems design work.

Both the old quad and the new quad describe the work I do, but the new quad is a better fit. My colleagues are used to hearing me talk about words. Now I need them to notice that I'm also doing systems work. So, I’ll explain that content strategy is a combination of content design and systems design. It connects our content work with our business goals.

### What I’m Working On

* Updating my job description to include content strategy.

### Question

* What’s a good way to describe content strategy? I need to make it easy for my boss and the rest of the company to start using the term.

### Answer

* Content strategy is a combination of content design and systems design. It connects content work with business goals.
